Section 01

Country Profile

France is a country in West Europe with a population of approximately 68 million people and it is home to the largest Muslim population in all of Western Europe. According to research from organizations such as the Pew Research Center and Institut Montaigne, there is an estimated 5 to 6 million Muslims that live in France. This statistic represents around 8-9% of the French population. Most French Muslims immigrate from former countries like Algeria, Morocco, and Tunisia.

An important feature to French society is laïcité, which is a model of securlarism established by the 1905 law that separated church and state. This law was meant to ensure religious neutrality in public institutions, however there are reports from Amnesty International and the Council of Europe that argue it to disproportionately affecting muslim communities in modern day.

1905

Law of Separation of Church and State

France's 1905 seperation of church state set the foundations forlaïcité, the French model of secularism. This is important because it later created debates over Muslim religious expression especially in regards to their clothing and public institutions.

1962

Algerian Independence & Mass Migration

After Algeria gained its Independence, migration from North Africa became the main cause for the Growth of Muslim communities in France. Institut Montaigne mentions Muslims in France often having family origins connected to countries like Algeria, Morocco, and Tunisia to name a few.

2004

Law Banning Religious Symbols in Schools

France bans "conspicuous" religious symbols in public schools in 2004. Amnesty Intenrationl argues that this type of ban is aimed mostly Muslim people especially those wearing hijabs.

2010

Full-Face Veil Ban

France became one of the first countries to full face coverings such as those by the niqab in public spaces. Amnesty International has opposed similar full-face veil bans, arguing that they violate freedom of religion and expression.

2015

State of Emergency After Paris Attacks

After the 2015 Paris attacks, France grew their emergency security personnel, however Human Rights Watch reported that these measures led to discriminartoy raids and house arrests against Muslims.

2021

Law Against "Separatism"

The Law Reinforcing Republican Principles, widely dubbed the "anti-separatism law," introduces sweeping measures critics say target Muslim associations, schools, and civil society organizations.

Section 02

Research on Islamophobia in France

Islamophobia in France operates across multiple levels, from everyday social interactions to institutionalized policies. Research from peer-reviewed journals, civil society reports, and governmental data demonstrates that these patterns are not accidental, but reflect a broader structural system. As discussed in course frameworks, this system functions through processes of “othering,” where Muslim communities are positioned as fundamentally different and subject to regulation through law, media, and public discourse.

📊

Hate Crime & Incident Data

Data from the CNCDH shows ongoing anti-Muslim incidents in France, including harassment, assaults, and vandalism of mosques. These incidents often rise after national security events, suggesting a connection between political narratives and public behavior. This shows that Islamophobia is not random, but reinforced by broader social and political forces.

Source: CNCDH, Islamophobia Studies Journal
⚖️

Structural Discrimination

Studies from Sciences Po show that applicants with Arabic-sounding names receive fewer job callbacks than others. This highlights bias in hiring, even when systems appear neutral. This reflects structural Islamophobia, where discrimination is built into institutions rather than just individual attitudes.

Source: Sciences Po, European Report
🏫

Education & Religious Expression

The 2004 ban on religious symbols in schools has disproportionately affected Muslim girls who wear the hijab. Research links this to increased exclusion and negative academic outcomes. This shows how laws based on secularism can still regulate Muslim identity and reinforce “othering.”

Source: ISJ, Amnesty International Report
🏘️

Housing & Urban Segregation

Many Muslim communities are concentrated in underfunded suburban areas known as banlieues, with high unemployment and limited resources. These conditions are linked to broader patterns of exclusion. This shows how Islamophobia connects to economic and urban inequality.

Source: Human Rights Watch, EU MIDIS Survey
🗳️

Political Rhetoric & Policy

Reports show that anti-Muslim rhetoric has become more common in French politics. Both far-right and mainstream leaders often frame Islam as incompatible with French identity. This helps shape public opinion and justify restrictive policies, reinforcing the idea of Muslims as a social “other.”

Source: European Islamophobia Report, ECRI Reports
🌐

Online Islamophobia

Muslim communities in France face widespread harassment online, including hate speech and targeted attacks. Reports show that social media amplifies these narratives. This shows how Islamophobia spreads beyond institutions into everyday digital spaces.

Source: Council of Europe,European Islamophobia Report

Section 04

Media, Film & Islamophobic Representation

Media plays a major role in shaping how Muslims are viewed in France. Films, news outlets, and online spaces often connect Muslim identity with danger, extremism, or failure to assimilate. This matters because these representations help normalize the idea that Muslims are outsiders in French society.

Films & Television

Film

La Désintégration (2011)

This film focuses on the radicalization of young Muslim men in France. While it tries to show a complex issue, it can also reinforce the idea that Muslim youth from the banlieues are naturally connected to extremism. This connects to the course idea of “othering,” where Muslims are shown as separate from the rest of society.

Television

Engrenages / Spiral (2005–2021)

This crime drama often places Arab and Muslim characters in stories about crime, policing, and suspicion. Even when the show is not openly Islamophobic, these repeated patterns can make Muslim communities seem dangerous or criminal. This shows how media can reinforce stereotypes without directly saying them.

Press, Print & Online

Print Media

Le Figaro & Valeurs Actuelles

Studies have shown that these outlets often frame Islam as a threat to French identity. Valeurs Actuelles has also faced legal action for racist depictions of Muslim figures.

Social Media

Online Islamophobic Ecosystems

Social media platforms allow Islamophobic content to spread quickly, but they also attempt to regulate it through content warnings and moderation tools. Posts related to Islam and politics are often flagged as sensitive, showing how these discussions are both widespread and controversial. This highlights how Islamophobia exists in everyday digital spaces, even when platforms try to limit its visibility.

Section 06

Economic Restrictions & Disparities

Muslim communities in France face economic challenges that go beyond individual situations. These patterns show up in hiring, banking, and mobility, and they are not random. They reflect broader systems where discrimination is built into institutions, which connects to the idea of structural Islamophobia.

Employment

  • CV discrimination: Arabic-sounding names receive significantly fewer callbacks (Sciences Po matched-pair study)
  • Higher unemployment rates in banlieues with large Muslim populations
  • Underrepresentation in senior public sector and corporate roles
  • Refusals of religious accommodation (prayer breaks, dress code) documented in workplaces
  • Discrimination complaints to the Défenseur des droits disproportionately involve Muslim complainants

    • Together, these patters show that even when systems appear neutral, Muslim applicants oftenface disadvantages in access to jobs and opportunities.

Banking & Business

  • Reported ethnic/religious profiling in bank account openings and credit assessments
  • Muslim entrepreneurs report disproportionate barriers to business loans
  • No state-recognised Islamic finance framework — halal mortgage products unavailable through major banks
  • Asset freezing under counter-terrorism legislation affects Muslim-owned businesses
  • Freezing of accounts linked to Muslim charities under "terrorism financing" designations

    • These patterns show how financial systems can also create barriers, making it harder for Muslim individuals and businesses to access resources.

Travel & Mobility

  • Selective border stops and secondary screening for Muslim travellers documented by civil liberties groups
  • Fiche S security designation restricts freedom of movement; overrepresentation of Muslims reported by journalists
  • No-fly lists and travel bans applied without criminal charge or judicial oversight
  • Visa restrictions and delays for nationals of Muslim-majority countries (Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia)
  • Deportation orders used against Muslim preachers and community leaders

    • These restrictions show how security policies can impact everyday movement and limit opportunities for certain groups.

Section 07

Surveillance, Emergency Laws & Terrorism Restrictions

France has expanded its surveillance and counter-terrorism policies, especially after 2015. These measures are presented as necessary for security, but research shows they often impact Muslim communities the most. This shows how security policies can create unequal effects in everyday life.

4,000+

State of Emergency Raids

Following the November 2015 Bataclan attack, France declared a state of emergency lasting nearly two years. Human Rights Watch documented over 4,000 warrantless raids, the vast majority targeting Muslim homes, mosques, and businesses, with fewer than 1% resulting in terrorism-related charges (Human Rights Watch).

SILT Law

Permanent Emergency Powers (2017)

The SILT Law made key emergency measures permanent, allowing mosque closures by prefectural decree, individual control orders without criminal conviction, and perimeter restrictions. Critics, including the Council of Europe's Venice Commission, noted insufficient judicial oversight (Council of Europe).

Fiche S

Security File Designations

The "Fiche S" system flags individuals deemed potential threats. Journalists and lawyers have documented overrepresentation of Muslim men, including those with no criminal record, and its use in restricting employment and travel (investigative reporting).

CNAPR

Counter-Radicalisation Infrastructure

France's counter-radicalisation system has been criticised for defining "radicalisation" so broadly that ordinary Muslim practices can be flagged as risk indicators. This chilling effect is documented in the Islamophobia Studies Journal (Islamophobia Studies Journal).

Final Section

Conclusion

This project demonstrates that Islamophobia in France is not limited to individual prejudice, but operates as a structural system across multiple domains. From legal restrictions to economic barriers and media representation, Muslim communities are consistently positioned as the “others” within French society. While these policies are often justified through secularism and security, their effects reveal patterns of exclusion that raise important ethical and social questions about equality, identity, and belonging in modern France.
